# FRANCHISE ARCHETYPES — The Lumenridge Faction Mapping
*Core worldbuilding document. This is the DNA of Franchise Underworld.*

---

## The Core Concept

**Franchise Underworld is set in the late 1990s.** Lumenridge is an amalgamation of American city aesthetics — part Houston, part Las Vegas, part Atlanta, part Anytown USA strip mall corridor. 

The **factions are the franchises.** Every restaurant chain that existed in late-90s America has been elevated into a political and criminal power structure. The "fine dining" tiers, the fast food tiers, the supplier tiers — they are the government, the military, the underworld.

The factions don't just *reference* these franchises. They **are** them — with their logos redesigned, their corporate culture metastasized into feudalism, their labor practices made literal law.

## THE FRANCHISE TIER HIERARCHY

### 🥩 TIER 1 — "Fine Dining" (The Board-Serving Class)
> *In 1997, these were the aspirational steakhouses. The birthday dinner. "Somewhere nice."*

| Lumenridge Faction | Real-World Archetype | Visual Signature |
|--------------------|---------------------|-----------------|
| **The Brand House** | Outback Steakhouse / Texas Roadhouse / Lone Star | Wood paneling, exposed brick, red vinyl booths, pendant lights, "No Rules, Just Right" propaganda posters |
| *(unnamed)* | Red Lobster / Olive Garden | Coastal/Italian aspirational, white tablecloths but plastic quality |

**Brand House Kitchen Reality:** Corporate recipe binders. Portion control scales. Vacuum-sealed frozen protein bags. A fryer that technically passes health inspection. The BOH is franchise-industrial, not European-chef pristine. The chaos is real but the tools are corporate.

**Brand House FOH Reality:** That specific late-90s chain warmth. Flair on the servers. Dim pendant lighting. Peanut shells on the floor (Texas Roadhouse). A family in their Sunday best celebrating a birthday at the big corner booth. The smell of blooming onion from the table over.

### 🍔 TIER 2 — "Casual Dining" (The Middle Class)
> *The everyday lunch. The after-church stop. The working family.*

| Lumenridge Faction | Real-World Archetype | Visual Signature |
|--------------------|---------------------|-----------------|
| **The Grill Houses** | McDonald's / Burger King / Wendy's | Corner lot supremacy, drive-thru timers, golden arches energy, neon on asphalt |
| *(unnamed)* | Applebee's / TGI Fridays / Chili's | Strip mall central, flair culture, the middle-management lunch |

**Grill House Reality:** Speed over safety. Sociopathic drive-thru timers. Grease burns as a rite of passage. The intersection corner lot is a political territory, not just a location.

---

### 🥚 TIER 3 — "Breakfast Empires" (The Dawn Faction)
> *The 24-hour shift feeders. The ones open when the others sleep.*

| Lumenridge Faction | Real-World Archetype | Visual Signature |
|--------------------|---------------------|-----------------|
| **The Morning Order** | Denny's / IHOP / Waffle House | Fluorescent 24/7 light, laminated menus, the 3am regulars, coffee that never empties |

**Morning Order Reality:** They never close. They serve the night shift workers, the insomniacs, the people coming down from something. The waitresses know everyone's name. The cook hasn't gone home since Tuesday.

---

### ⛽ TIER 4 — "The Supply Chain" (The Invisible Power)
> *You never see them. You eat what they allow.*

| Lumenridge Faction | Real-World Archetype | Visual Signature |
|--------------------|---------------------|-----------------|
| **Ghost Oil Refineries** | Sysco / US Foods / the supplier industrial complex | Tanker trucks, refrigerated warehouses, men in suits with clipboards |

**Ghost Oil Reality:** They don't serve food. They control who gets to serve food. Every Brand House, every Grill House, every Morning Order franchise — they all answer to Ghost Oil for their raw materials. The refineries are the real government of Lumenridge.

## VISUAL LANGUAGE IMPLICATIONS

Because the setting is **1997 American franchise**, the visual language must reflect:

- **Wood paneling** and exposed brick (not sleek futurism)
- **Neon tube signage** (bent glass, not LED)
- **Laminated menus** and paper placemats
- **Flair** (buttons, pins, suspenders on servers)
- **Vinyl booths** in red or forest green
- **Overhead pendant lights** with warm amber bulbs
- **Carpeted dining rooms** in chain restaurants (yes, carpet)
- **Frosted glass dividers** between sections
- The **parking lot** as a social space (fights, deals, cigarettes)

The dystopian element is NOT in the aesthetics looking sci-fi or dark. It's in the **political power** these mundane spaces hold. A Waffle House that controls who eats in a 10-block radius. An Outback with its own security force. That dissonance — the banal familiarity of the setting vs the brutal power structures within it — IS the horror.
